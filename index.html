History of the Android Mascot

You probably know that the Android operating system, maintained by Google, is one of the most widely used for mobile devices worldwide. But maybe you don't know that its friendly mascot has a name and a very curious history? So, follow this article to learn a lot about this little robot.

The First Version

The first attempt to create a mascot emerged in 2007 and came from a developer named Dan Morrill. He explains that he opened Inkscape (a free vector graphics software) and created his own version of a robot. The goal was merely to personify the system for his team; there was no request from the company for the creation of a mascot.

This first bizarre version was even named after its creator: the Dandroids.

A new mascot emerges

The idea of having a mascot evolved, and the task was handed over to a professional in the field. Russian illustrator Irina Blok, also an employee of Google, was given the mission to represent the little robot in a more appealing way.

Irina's main idea was to represent everything graphically with few lines and in a more flat style. The design should also allow for quick recognition by those who see it. Thus, the Bugdroid emerged, the new mascot of Android.

The main inspiration for the lines of the new Bugdroid came from those little figures that illustrate bathroom doors to indicate gender. Legend has it that the artist was working at her desk at Google and glanced at the bathroom signs, and the identification was immediate: simple, clean, and to the point.

Want to learn more?

Another interesting fact about Android is that each version has always been named after a dessert, in alphabetical order from version 1.5 up to 9.0.

  • 1.5 - Cupcake
  • 1.6 - Donut
  • 3.0 - Eclair
  • 2.2 - Froyo
  • 2.3 - Gingerbread
  • 3.0 - Honeycomb
  • 4.0 - Ice Cream Sandwich
  • 4.1 - Jelly Bean
  • 4.4 - KitKat
  • 5.0 - Lolipop
  • 6.0 - Marshmallow
  • 7.0 - Nougat
  • 8.0 - Oreo
  • 9.0 - Pie

Unfortunately, Android Q did not exist, as Google decided to end this fun practice and began using numerical versions, which led to Android 10.

Visit the Android History website to explore the sequence of 'sweet' versions and what each one brought to the Android system.
